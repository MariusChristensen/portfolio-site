Case Studies

Improving a Sales Product Page

1. Context

The company my girlfriend works for wanted to improve their product pages for better usability and conversions. She asked if I could take a look and give some suggestions — so I did a quick review and mockup.

2. Problem

The current product pages had:

  • Lots of unnecessary whitespace
  • Weak visual hierarchy (important elements weren't standing out)
  • Way too many buttons (e.g., 20+ individual options shown as buttons)
  • Inefficient layout and poor use of space

3. Goals

My main focus was to:

  • Reduce visual clutter
  • Emphasize key actions (like "Add to quotation request")
  • Simplify UI components (like turning 20 buttons into a dropdown)
  • Improve overall readability and layout

4. My Process

  • Gave the page a quick UX audit (gut check + basic heuristics)
  • Sketched a low-fidelity wireframe to communicate layout ideas
  • Sent the wireframe through my girlfriend to her boss
  • Feedback: he really liked it — saw potential and appreciated the simplicity

5. Before & After

Created a low-fidelity wireframe that transformed the cluttered original layout into a clean, focused design with improved visual hierarchy and streamlined user interactions.

Before
After

6. What I Learned

  • Even a quick UX pass can uncover big wins
  • Communicating ideas visually (even low-fidelity) works great
  • Small layout and flow tweaks can improve the user journey a lot

Turning My CV Into a Website

1. Context

I looked at my old, outdated CV — the kind you'd make in Word — and immediately hated the idea of rebuilding it the same way. It felt rigid, uninspired, and didn't reflect what I actually do: build things with code.

So I figured... why not code my CV?

2. The Why

  • I wanted full control over layout and styling
  • Coding is more fun than dragging boxes in Word
  • I needed a CV that was easy to update, customize, and evolve over time

3. The Approach

  • Built with clean HTML, CSS, and a bit of JavaScript
  • Took layout inspiration from other dev CVs online
  • Kept the structure simple and readable — no fancy frameworks, just good ol' semantic markup

4. Features

  • Language toggle for English/Norwegian, since this industry often goes beyond Norwegian borders
  • Print button to easily export a PDF version when someone wants it on paper
  • QR codes for GitHub and portfolio links, since hyperlinks don't exactly work on paper
  • Hosted on Netlify for easy sharing with a custom link
  • Mobile-friendly-ish — works fine on smaller screens, though it's mainly desktop-first

5. What I Learned

Honestly? It wasn't a technically complex project. But:

  • It showed how even small projects can solve real problems
  • Print styling is weird — especially on Chromium browsers (lookin' at you, random bolded hyphens)
  • The process reminded me that design isn't just about looks — it's about usefulness

6. The Impact

  • Got great feedback from mentors and peers
  • A few people suggested I turn it into a proper case study (so... here we are)
  • I've even been asked to present it to others in similar situations

7. Refinements

After sharing the project and getting both helpful and critical feedback, I decided to make a few targeted changes to improve clarity and usability.

  • Kept only the language toggle visible in the public version — it's the one feature that made sense for external viewers
  • Moved everything else into a hidden "admin mode" — the extra options were meant for me, but confused others
  • It's now a clean, dedicated online CV I can confidently share without worrying about alternate versions
